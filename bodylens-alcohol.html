It is a psychoactive compound that the body treats as a toxic priority fuel — metabolising it before everything else, while disrupting sleep architecture, hormonal output, muscle protein synthesis, gut integrity, and fat oxidation simultaneously. The question is not whether it has these effects. It does. The question is which effects matter most at your doses and your timing, and what you can actually do about it.

The mechanism — why ethanol changes everything else you eat
Before examining specific effects, the fundamental metabolic fact that makes alcohol uniquely disruptive — and why it cannot be treated like food.
1
Ethanol is a metabolic priority fuel — the body stops everything else to process it first
Alcohol (ethanol) is toxic to cells. The moment it enters the bloodstream, the liver treats its clearance as an emergency — pausing normal metabolic processes to process it via alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and acetaldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH). While alcohol is being metabolised, fat oxidation is essentially halted. The liver is busy converting ethanol to acetaldehyde (toxic) then to acetate. Fat cannot be burned in meaningful quantities while this is happening. Carbohydrates continue to be metabolised normally, but dietary fat consumed with alcohol is preferentially shuttled to storage rather than oxidation — a mechanism that explains why the meal you eat with drinks contributes more to fat storage than the same meal without them.

Alcohol provides 7 kcal per gram — more than carbohydrates (4 kcal/g) and more than protein (4 kcal/g), slightly less than fat (9 kcal/g). But unlike food macronutrients, alcohol cannot be stored — it must be processed immediately, which is why it dominates metabolism for its entire clearance window.
Alcohol = metabolic emergency · fat oxidation pauses · fat storage increases during the clearance window
2
Acetaldehyde — the intermediate metabolite that causes most of the damage
Ethanol → acetaldehyde → acetate. The middle step is where most cellular damage occurs. Acetaldehyde is far more toxic than ethanol itself — it binds to proteins, creates DNA adducts, disrupts mitochondrial function, and generates reactive oxygen species (ROS). The inflammation, muscle damage, and cellular disruption associated with alcohol are primarily acetaldehyde effects, not ethanol effects. The speed at which you can convert acetaldehyde to the relatively harmless acetate depends on your ALDH2 gene variant — those with reduced ALDH2 activity accumulate acetaldehyde faster, producing the flushing response and disproportionate cellular damage at the same alcohol dose. If you flush easily from alcohol, your acetaldehyde accumulation is higher at every drink.
Acetaldehyde is the primary damage agent · DNA damage · mitochondrial disruption · the hangover
3
Clearance rate is fixed — roughly one standard drink per hour regardless of coffee, food, or time
The liver processes approximately 1 standard UK unit (8g ethanol) per hour. This rate cannot be meaningfully accelerated by food, caffeine, water, cold showers, or exercise. A large glass of wine (250ml, 13% ABV) is approximately 3 units — taking 3 hours to clear. During this entire window, the metabolic disruptions described above are active. Understanding clearance rate matters for timing: drinking at 10pm means clearance completes around 1am for 3 units, 3am for 5 units. If GH pulses during slow-wave sleep occur in the first sleep cycle (typically 90 minutes after sleep onset), alcohol consumed within 4–5 hours of sleep will suppress them regardless of when you fell asleep.
~1 unit/hour clearance · timing is the most important variable you can control

Muscle & MPS — the mTOR story
The most direct fitness effect of alcohol — and the one most people don't know about. It is not about calories. It is about the molecular switch that determines whether your training session produces muscle growth.
1
Alcohol directly inhibits mTOR — the molecular switch for muscle protein synthesis
mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) is the primary intracellular signal that initiates muscle protein synthesis following training and protein intake. Leucine from protein activates it. The training stimulus activates it. And ethanol directly suppresses its phosphorylation — independently of calories, sleep quality, or any other variable. This is not an indirect effect. Ethanol reaches skeletal muscle tissue directly and blocks the mTOR signalling cascade that your training session just activated. Alcohol on a training day effectively tells the muscle to ignore the stimulus it just received. The work happened. The adaptation was not allowed to begin.
Ethanol blocks mTOR directly · not a calorie or sleep effect · a direct molecular interference

2
The 24-hour window — the MPS suppression outlasts the alcohol
MPS suppression from alcohol does not resolve when the alcohol is metabolised. The mTOR inhibition from 3–5 drinks post-training persists for approximately 24 hours — meaning that the session you trained on Monday, followed by 3 drinks Monday evening, has reduced adaptation not just Monday night but through Tuesday. Wednesday's training session begins from a lower anabolic baseline than it would have without Monday's drinking. This compounding effect is why consistent regular drinking on training days produces worse results than the same volume of alcohol on rest days — the 24-hour suppression window overlaps with the next session's stimulus.
MPS suppression lasts ~24h post-drinking · compounds across consecutive training days
3
Protein before drinking — the most important mitigation strategy
Consuming 40g of fast-absorbing protein (whey) within 30 minutes of training, before the first drink, achieves two things. First, it initiates the MPS window with maximal leucine delivery before ethanol can suppress mTOR. Second, protein slows gastric emptying, reducing the speed of alcohol absorption and lowering peak blood alcohol concentration. Research by Parr et al. (2014) specifically demonstrated that the post-training protein dose partially mitigates alcohol's MPS suppression — not eliminating it, but reducing the degree of inhibition. The practical rule is binary: protein before the first drink, every time, always. Not protein and alcohol together — protein first, then alcohol after the post-workout window is initiated.
40g protein immediately post-training · before first drink · partially protects the MPS window
Sleep destruction — sedation is not sleep
This is alcohol's most underappreciated fitness effect and arguably its most damaging one. Alcohol makes falling asleep easier and makes sleep far less useful simultaneously.
1
Alcohol sedates — it does not produce sleep. The architecture is completely different.
Normal sleep cycles through NREM stages 1, 2, 3 (slow-wave/deep sleep) and REM in roughly 90-minute cycles. Deep sleep (NREM stage 3) is when GH is released and muscle repair occurs. REM is when memory consolidation and emotional processing happen. Alcohol dramatically suppresses REM sleep in the first half of the night and fragments sleep in the second half as it is metabolised. The person who "slept 8 hours" after 4 drinks has spent that time in a sedated, shallow sleep state — not the restorative architecture of true sleep. Subjectively they may feel they slept; objectively, their HRV will be depressed, GH will not have pulsed adequately, and muscle repair will have been severely limited.
Sedation ≠ sleep · 8h with alcohol ≠ 8h sleep · GH pulse suppressed · REM absent

2
Growth hormone is suppressed even when deep sleep appears present
The deep sleep that occurs in the first half of an alcohol-affected night is not GH-productive sleep. Acetaldehyde and alcohol metabolites directly suppress hypothalamic GHRH (growth hormone releasing hormone), which is what triggers the GH pulse during slow-wave sleep. The architecture may look correct on a sleep tracker — the body is in NREM stage 3 — but the GH is not being released because the hypothalamus is suppressed. This means the overnight muscle repair window, connective tissue remodelling, and body composition benefits of quality sleep are all compromised even on nights where total sleep time appears normal. A sleep tracker cannot detect this; it can only see sleep stages, not hormonal output.
Deep sleep stage present ≠ GH releasing · acetaldehyde suppresses the hypothalamic signal
3
The 4-hour rule — minimum time between last drink and sleep for partial sleep protection
The clearance rate of ~1 unit/hour means that 3 units (approximately 1.5 large glasses of wine) require about 3 hours to clear. Drinking finishing at least 3–4 hours before sleep allows the first sleep cycle to occur with substantially reduced blood alcohol — partially preserving the first GH pulse. Finishing 2 drinks at 8pm and sleeping at midnight gives approximately 4 hours of clearance — meaningfully better than 2 drinks at 10pm and sleeping at midnight. The practical implication: start earlier, finish earlier. The same quantity of alcohol produces significantly less sleep disruption when clearance is more complete before sleep onset.
Finish drinking 3–4h before bed · same amount of alcohol · meaningfully better sleep
Hormones — testosterone, cortisol, oestrogen
Alcohol's hormonal effects are meaningful at 44 specifically — when the testosterone decline from the previous decade makes every percentage point of further suppression consequential.
Testosterone suppression
Alcohol reduces testosterone through three simultaneous pathways
1. Leydig cell suppression: Ethanol directly impairs the testicular Leydig cells that produce testosterone. Even moderate drinking (3–4 units/day for several days) measurably reduces testosterone production at the source.

2. Cortisol elevation: Alcohol raises cortisol significantly, particularly during the clearance window. Cortisol and testosterone are inversely related — elevated cortisol suppresses the HPT (hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular) axis, reducing LH release and therefore testosterone production.

3. Increased aromatisation: Alcohol activates aromatase — the enzyme that converts testosterone to oestradiol (a form of oestrogen). The net result is reduced testosterone AND elevated oestrogen simultaneously.

The acute effect on a single heavy-drinking night: testosterone can fall 20–25% within 12 hours and remains suppressed for up to 24 hours after clearance. The chronic effect from regular moderate drinking (14+ units/week): consistently lower testosterone baseline over weeks and months.
At 44, testosterone is already declining at 1–2% annually. Alcohol accelerates the decline with every drinking session.
Cortisol
Alcohol spikes cortisol — directly competing with the anabolic environment you're building
Alcohol consumption raises cortisol via HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis activation. The cortisol spike from a heavy drinking session can reach 200–400% of baseline during the clearance window — producing the same physiological state as acute psychological stress. Elevated cortisol promotes visceral fat accumulation, suppresses testosterone and GH simultaneously, drives muscle protein catabolism, and impairs insulin sensitivity. This is why the combination of training stress (appropriate acute cortisol) and post-training alcohol (additional cortisol) is particularly damaging — you are stacking two cortisol signals at the time when you most need cortisol to return to baseline to allow adaptation.
Ashwagandha reduces cortisol by 25–30% — helpful on non-drinking days but insufficient to counter heavy-drinking cortisol spikes.
Oestrogen and aromatisation
The oestrogen effect — under-discussed and directly relevant at 44
Alcohol activates aromatase — most significantly in the liver and adipose tissue. As testosterone is aromatised to oestradiol, the net hormonal environment shifts: lower testosterone, higher oestrogen. In men, elevated oestrogen promotes fat storage (particularly subcutaneous fat rather than visceral), reduces muscle hardness and definition, and at high levels can produce gynecomastia. This is dose-dependent and chronic rather than acute — regular moderate-to-heavy drinking over months produces a measurable shift in the testosterone-to-oestrogen ratio that directly affects body composition outcomes. The effect compounds with the natural age-related aromatase activity increase in the 40s.
Regular alcohol consumption shifts the T:E ratio unfavourably — a body composition effect that operates independently of calories.
Insulin sensitivity
Acute improvement, chronic impairment — alcohol's contradictory insulin effect
The research on alcohol and insulin is paradoxical. Light-to-moderate drinking (1–2 drinks) is associated with improved insulin sensitivity in epidemiological data — part of the widely cited cardiovascular benefit of moderate drinking. However, heavy drinking and chronic regular drinking impair insulin sensitivity through pancreatic stress, liver glycogen dysregulation, and acetaldehyde-induced mitochondrial damage. For a trained individual focused on performance, the insulin sensitivity benefit of moderate drinking is real but modest — and is dwarfed by the sleep, MPS, and hormonal effects in the context of an active training programme.
The cardiovascular epidemiology of moderate drinking applies to sedentary populations. For trained individuals, the negative effects of the same dose are disproportionately larger.
Fat & body composition — it's not just the calories
Alcohol's body composition effects go beyond its caloric content. The metabolic priority it demands changes what happens to every other macronutrient consumed with it.
1
Dietary fat consumed with alcohol is preferentially stored — not because of calories but because of fat oxidation suppression
While the liver processes ethanol (up to 3–4 hours for 3–4 drinks), its capacity for beta-oxidation (fat burning) is severely reduced. Dietary fat consumed during this window cannot be oxidised for energy at normal rates — it circulates as triglycerides and is progressively stored in adipose tissue. The high-fat meal consumed with alcohol (the steak, the cheese, the chips) is metabolically far more lipogenic than the same meal without alcohol. The alcohol is not "using" the fat for energy — it is displacing fat metabolism entirely. This is one of the primary mechanisms behind the body composition cost of regular social drinking even at relatively moderate caloric intake.
Fat + alcohol = fat storage. The alcohol meal is the worst time to eat high fat.
2
Alcohol activates appetite — through multiple independent pathways simultaneously
Alcohol disinhibits eating behaviour through cortical suppression (reduced self-monitoring) while simultaneously triggering physiological hunger signals: ghrelin rises acutely with alcohol consumption, and the AGRP neurons in the hypothalamus — the primary hunger-driving neural population — are directly activated by ethanol. This is the mechanism behind the "alcohol makes you hungry" experience — it is not just lowered inhibition, it is genuine hormonal hunger activation combined with reduced satiety awareness. The late-night kebab or post-pub snacking represents both a physiological hunger signal (ghrelin, AGRP) and a disinhibited response to it — a double mechanism that consistently adds untracked calories. Studies show people consume an average of 350–400 additional calories on drinking occasions beyond the alcohol itself.
Alcohol activates ghrelin AND disinhibits eating · average +350 kcal from food on drinking occasions
3
NEAT collapse the day after drinking — the hidden caloric cost
HRV (heart rate variability) is the most sensitive marker of nervous system recovery — and it is severely depressed after a heavy drinking session. The low-HRV state the day after drinking produces a measurable reduction in NEAT: you sit more, move less, and unconsciously conserve energy in the same way that sleep deprivation reduces NEAT. A night of 5+ drinks can reduce next-day NEAT by 200–400 kcal — a hidden caloric cost that is never attributed to the drinking but is mechanistically caused by it. Combined with the appetite activation (additional food consumed while drinking) and the caloric content of the drinks themselves, the total body composition cost of a heavy-drinking night frequently exceeds 1,500–2,000 kcal when all three components are accounted for.
Next-day NEAT drops 200–400 kcal · the hidden cost that never gets counted
Gut & inflammation — the system that runs everything else
Relevant to you specifically — because your omega-3 and anti-inflammatory protocol is doing careful work that alcohol can disrupt.
Intestinal permeability
Alcohol increases gut permeability — the "leaky gut" mechanism
Alcohol disrupts tight junction proteins (occludin, claudins) in the intestinal epithelium — the molecular seals between intestinal cells. When these break down, bacterial endotoxins (LPS — lipopolysaccharides) from gut bacteria can translocate into the bloodstream. LPS in the bloodstream triggers a systemic inflammatory response via TLR4 (toll-like receptor 4) on immune cells — producing the malaise, systemic inflammation, and immune activation associated with heavy drinking. This effect occurs even at moderate doses (2–3 drinks) and compounds with regular drinking. The resulting systemic inflammation directly impairs muscle recovery, worsens joint inflammation, and disrupts the gut microbiome balance.
This is the mechanism behind "feeling off" the day after moderate drinking even without a hangover.
COX pathway disruption — direct relevance to your knee
Alcohol counteracts your omega-3 anti-inflammatory protocol
Your omega-3 supplementation (3g+ EPA/DHA) works by competing with arachidonic acid for COX-1 and COX-2 enzymes, reducing prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) — the primary inflammatory mediator in synovial tissue. Alcohol disrupts this in two ways: first, alcohol directly upregulates COX-2 expression through NFkB activation, producing more of the enzyme your omega-3 is trying to down-regulate. Second, alcohol increases arachidonic acid availability through phospholipase A2 activation — increasing the substrate that competes with your EPA/DHA. A drinking session can partially negate several days of omega-3 anti-inflammatory work. For the meniscal environment specifically, this means the joint protection protocol is most vulnerable in the 24–48 hours following a drinking session.
Drinking partially negates your omega-3 protocol · the meniscal environment is most vulnerable 24–48h post-drinking.
Microbiome
Chronic drinking reshapes the gut bacteria — away from the profile that supports training
The gut microbiome composition associated with optimal immune function, reduced systemic inflammation, and efficient nutrient absorption is measurably different from the microbiome of regular moderate-to-heavy drinkers. Regular alcohol consumption reduces Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus populations (anti-inflammatory, associated with better mood and reduced anxiety) and increases Gram-negative bacteria (producers of the LPS endotoxin described above). Your probiotic-supporting foods (Greek yoghurt, sourdough, fibre-rich vegetables) are building the microbiome you want — and regular alcohol is selectively degrading it.
Recovery: fermented foods (yoghurt, kefir) and diverse plant fibre consumption are the fastest microbiome restoration tools after a heavy session.
The hangover — what is actually happening
A hangover is not dehydration — it is multi-system toxicity
The popular "hangover = dehydration" framework is incomplete. Dehydration plays a role (alcohol is a diuretic via ADH suppression) but accounts for perhaps 20% of the symptoms. The rest: acetaldehyde toxicity (the primary agent — most hangover symptoms correlate with acetaldehyde accumulation rate); glutamate rebound (alcohol suppresses glutamate receptors; as alcohol clears, glutamate surges, producing anxiety, sensitivity to light and sound, and inability to sleep in the second half of the night); prostaglandin-mediated inflammation (the headache, body ache, and nausea are prostaglandin E2 effects); and hypoglycaemia (alcohol suppresses gluconeogenesis, producing the low blood sugar that drives fatigue and food craving the morning after).
Best hangover protocol: 500ml electrolyte water on waking, carbohydrates for the hypoglycaemia, rest (not training through a hangover — cortisol + dehydration + already impaired MPS = no net benefit).
Practical framework — how to navigate alcohol without losing ground
This is not a prohibition framework. It is a damage management system built on the mechanisms above. The goal: enjoy alcohol when you choose to, minimise its impact on the things you care about, and understand clearly what the trade-offs are.
Always avoid — non-negotiable
Alcohol on training days. The mTOR suppression, testosterone reduction, and cortisol spike land exactly when the training adaptation window needs to be open. The session is partially or fully wasted.
Alcohol within 3–4 hours of sleep. The sleep architecture destruction is the worst long-term body composition cost of drinking. This alone is the primary reason to front-load any drinking.
Alcohol the night before a hard session. Wednesday's sleep quality determines Thursday's hormonal recovery for Friday. Friday's session is the week's most important.
Drinking through a hangover. Hair of the dog extends the acetaldehyde exposure and delays full recovery without meaningful relief.
When and how — the optimal window
Sunday is the optimal drinking day — furthest from the next training session (Monday), furthest from Friday's critical session, and the one-rule flexibility day is already built into the programme.
Start early, finish early. 4pm–8pm is the optimal drinking window — maximises clearance time before sleep, minimises sleep architecture disruption.
Tuesday is the next best option — between Monday (push) and Wednesday (pull), with Thursday as a full rest day to absorb any hormonal disruption before Friday.
Lower is always better. 2–3 units produces dramatically less disruption than 5–6. Each additional drink is a non-linear step up in damage, not a linear one.
The drinking session protocol — before, during, after
Before
Protein first — then drink
40g protein meal (chicken, fish, Greek yoghurt, whey) 30–60 minutes before the first drink. This does two things: slows gastric emptying and alcohol absorption (lower peak BAC from the same number of drinks), and ensures MPS is initiated before ethanol can suppress it on training days. A high-protein meal before drinking is one of the highest-leverage single interventions available and takes no willpower — it is just eating before you go out.
Choosing your drink
Lower ABV · avoid sugary mixers · avoid dark spirits
Lower ABV for the same volume = fewer units = less acetaldehyde accumulation. Dark spirits (whisky, bourbon, brandy, dark rum) contain congeners — additional toxic alcohols and aldehydes produced during fermentation that worsen acetaldehyde accumulation and hangover severity. Vodka, gin, and white wine have the lowest congener content. Sugary mixers add empty calories and produce a glucose spike that worsens next-morning hypoglycaemia. Soda water or tonic (not sugar tonic) with spirits, or dry wine, minimise this effect.
During
Alternate with water · eat before you feel hungry · pace to 1 unit/hour
Alternating water between drinks reduces total consumption naturally and maintains some hydration. Eat before the alcohol-induced hunger signal arrives — once ghrelin and AGRP neurons are activated by alcohol, the appetite is physiological and not manageable through willpower. Eating a protein-rich snack early prevents the late-session overeating that accounts for most of the dietary damage of a drinking occasion. Pacing to 1 unit/hour keeps blood alcohol lower throughout, reducing acetaldehyde accumulation.
Before bed
500ml water + electrolytes · no sleep aids
Electrolyte water (or a pinch of salt in water) addresses the diuresis component of the hangover. Do not take sleeping tablets or antihistamines with alcohol — the additive CNS depressant effect increases acetaldehyde accumulation and worsens sleep architecture disruption. Magnesium glycinate (already in your stack) is safe to take as normal. Sleep position: lateral rather than supine improves glymphatic clearance.
Morning after
Electrolytes · carbs · no training if HRV is down
500ml electrolyte water immediately. Carbohydrate breakfast (oats, banana, sourdough) to address the alcohol-induced hypoglycaemia — this is the mechanism behind the carb craving the morning after drinking. Protein normal amounts. Do not train if HRV is significantly depressed — training through a hangover state adds cortisol to an already cortisol-elevated environment, produces disproportionate inflammation, and generates essentially no anabolic adaptation. Rest and recovery produce better net results than a compromised training session.
What "moderation" actually means for a trained athlete
Optimal for training progress
0–4 units per week, on rest days, finishing 3+ hours before sleep
At this level, the measurable impact on weekly MPS, testosterone, and sleep quality is modest. The cumulative compounding over 12 weeks is present but small. A single glass of wine with Sunday dinner, finished by 8pm, has negligible impact on Monday's training performance or hormonal baseline. This is the framework most compatible with good training progress.
Manageable — diminishing returns
5–10 units per week, on rest days, with damage mitigation applied
Progress continues but measurably slower. Sleep quality is reliably impaired on drinking nights. Testosterone baseline is noticeably lower over the week. At this level, the programme still works — just at 70–80% of its potential. The gap between what you are achieving and what you could achieve is real and quantifiable.
Where it actively works against the programme
10+ units per week, or any drinking on training days
At this level, the hormonal environment (testosterone suppressed, cortisol chronically elevated, GH pulses disrupted) is sufficiently impaired that the programme's outcomes are meaningfully compromised. The training stimulus and the nutritional architecture are both present, but the hormonal environment required to actualise the adaptations they signal is not. This is where the gap between effort and outcome becomes demoralising and confusing — the work is happening, the results are not coming, and the alcohol is the reason.
