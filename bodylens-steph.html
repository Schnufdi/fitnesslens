The science — female physiology, perimenopause, gut-brain axis, and the sleep cascade
Perimenopause — what is actually happening
Oestrogen doesn't just decline — it fluctuates. The fluctuation is the problem.
Most people think of menopause as oestrogen declining smoothly to a lower level. Perimenopause — which can begin 8–10 years before menopause, and at 38–39 is entirely within normal range — is different. Oestrogen spikes erratically, sometimes to levels higher than normal, then crashes suddenly. These spikes cause the night sweats and sleep disruption even when no obvious sweating occurs — the brain's temperature regulation is disrupted by the oestrogen spike. The crashes cause the low mood, joint aching, cognitive fog, and emotional reactivity.

Progesterone declines more steadily, and progesterone is the body's primary GABA activator — the calming neurotransmitter. Lower progesterone means the nervous system is chronically more anxious, sleep architecture is disrupted (less slow-wave sleep), and the cortisol response to stressors is larger and longer. This is why a stressful day at work hits harder than it used to. The buffer is thinner.
The gut-brain axis — post-resection
The colon resection changed the gut's relationship with the brain.
The enteric nervous system — 500 million neurons lining the gut — communicates with the brain via the vagus nerve, predominantly upward (gut signals the brain, not the other way around). The gut produces 95% of the body's serotonin, regulates 70% of the immune system, and directly influences cortisol baseline and anxiety levels.

After colon resection, three things change: the mechanical transit rhythm of the gut is altered (explaining the cramping at unexpected times), the microbial ecosystem is disrupted (the bacteria removed with the tissue don't immediately repopulate in the same pattern), and the vagal signalling from the affected section is reduced. The result is lower serotonin output, higher baseline anxiety, impaired decision-making under stress, and digestive unpredictability. None of this is permanent — the gut is the most regenerable organ — but it requires active intervention, not just time.
Sleep architecture in perimenopause
The sleep problem is hormonal first, behavioural second.
Normal sleep architecture requires: stable oestrogen (night-time spikes cause waking), adequate progesterone (GABA activation for slow-wave sleep), cortisol nadir overnight (cortisol can't reach its low point when chronically elevated), and melatonin onset (suppressed by light exposure and cortisol).

In perimenopause, all four of these are compromised simultaneously. The result is the pattern Steph describes: difficulty falling asleep, waking in the night, eventually falling asleep in the early hours, and not getting enough total sleep. This is not insomnia in the traditional sense — it is a hormonal disruption of the sleep architecture systems. The fix is partly hormonal, partly environmental, and partly circadian anchor-setting.

The 2 hours of sleep last night has a specific consequence: cortisol never reached its overnight nadir. Today's cortisol baseline is elevated. Elevated cortisol suppresses appetite at some points and drives cravings at others, impairs decision-making (contributing to the food paralysis), and produces the physical fatigue that feels like a lead blanket.
The HPA axis under compound stress
The body's stress response system is running on empty.
The HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis produces cortisol in response to stress. It is designed for acute stressors with recovery between them. What Steph has experienced in the last 12 months — cancer diagnosis and surgery, new stressful role, shoulder injury, sleep deprivation, food anxiety — is a sustained period of HPA activation with insufficient recovery.

The result is adrenal fatigue — not in the pseudoscientific sense, but in the genuine physiological sense: the cortisol system becomes dysregulated, producing too much at the wrong times and too little at others. The "can't get up" feeling despite wanting to, the afternoon crashes, the inability to push through the gym session — these are not motivation failures. They are HPA dysregulation. Ashwagandha (KSM-66) is the most evidence-backed natural HPA modulator available, and it is the single most important supplement in Steph's stack.
Female testosterone — the missing conversation
Women need testosterone. And perimenopause depletes it.
Testosterone is not just a male hormone. In women, it drives: motivation and drive, muscle tone and lean mass, libido, cognitive sharpness, and the feeling of being "in the body" rather than outside looking in. Testosterone in women peaks in the mid-20s and begins declining through the 30s — but perimenopause accelerates this significantly. The "I don't feel like my body is my own" description Steph gave is a clinically recognised symptom of testosterone decline in perimenopausal women.

This is not fixed by supplements alone — it requires a GP conversation about hormonal assessment. But it informs every other part of the plan: resistance training (the strongest non-pharmaceutical testosterone stimulus in women), sleep (testosterone synthesised overnight), and stress reduction (cortisol is inversely related to testosterone).

1
Morning light — the most powerful circadian anchor available
The circadian clock is currently phase-delayed — Steph's biology thinks the day starts at midday, not at 7:30am. This is not a character trait, it is a clock that has drifted in response to the sleep disruption pattern.

The fix: within 30 minutes of waking, go outside for 10 minutes and look toward the sky (not directly at the sun). No sunglasses. The retinal ganglion cells that detect morning light signal the suprachiasmatic nucleus — the master circadian clock — that the day has started. This triggers: the cortisol awakening response at the correct time, serotonin synthesis that converts to melatonin that evening, and the beginning of the sleep pressure that will allow earlier sleep onset tonight.

Done consistently for 7–10 days, morning light shifts the circadian clock forward measurably. It is free, takes 10 minutes, and is the most impactful single habit for the sleep problem.

Gut & food — rebuilding the system and removing the decision

Post-resection gut — what's actually happening
Cramping after plain chicken is the gut-brain axis misfiring, not food intolerance.
Colon resection alters three things simultaneously: the mechanical transit rhythm (contractions that move food through are disrupted in the section around the resection site), the microbial ecosystem (bacteria that lived in the resected tissue don't immediately repopulate), and the enteric nervous system signalling (the section of gut that was removed loses its neural contribution to the motility coordination upstream and downstream).

Crucially: eating while stressed — which Steph is, chronically — activates the sympathetic nervous system, which diverts blood away from the gut and inhibits digestive motility. This is the mechanism behind stress-induced IBS flares. The plain chicken itself is not the problem. The state in which it is eaten may be. Eating in a calm, seated, slow state with no screens is a direct digestive intervention — not a wellness suggestion.
Microbiome rebuilding — the specific inputs
The gut can be rebuilt. It requires specific, consistent, deliberate inputs.
Probiotic: High-quality multi-strain probiotic containing both Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species. Take first thing in the morning on an empty stomach or with a small amount of food. Eating posture and state — underrated and real
How you eat matters as much as what you eat right now.
The parasympathetic nervous system (rest-and-digest) needs to be dominant for digestion to work properly. The sympathetic system (fight-or-flight) shuts digestion down — blood is diverted away from the gut, motility slows, gastric acid secretion decreases. Eating while anxious, rushed, or distracted is a direct digestive impairment, not just a bad habit.

The protocol for the post-resection gut: sit down, put the phone away, take 3 slow breaths before starting, eat slowly. This is not mindfulness as an aesthetic — it is a physiological switch from sympathetic to parasympathetic that changes what the gut can do with the food it receives. For Steph's gut specifically, this single habit change may reduce the cramping more than any individual food change.

Hormones — what to know, what to ask for, what to do
The GP conversation — what to request
Book this week. Request a full hormonal panel.
The following blood panel will give a complete picture of what is actually happening rather than what is estimated. Request specifically:

FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) and LH (luteinising hormone) — the primary perimenopause markers. FSH rises as the ovaries become less responsive.

Oestradiol (E2) — the active form of oestrogen. In perimenopause this fluctuates — a single reading may not capture the full picture, but it is a starting point.

Progesterone — taken on day 21 of the cycle if cycles are still regular. Declining progesterone is the primary driver of the sleep disruption and anxiety.

Total and free testosterone — frequently overlooked in women. The "don't feel like myself" symptom is often testosterone-driven.

Thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4) — thyroid dysfunction is common in perimenopausal women and produces an almost identical symptom profile to perimenopause itself: fatigue, weight changes, brain fog, low mood.

Vitamin D, ferritin (iron stores), B12, and CRP (inflammation marker) — these four can each individually produce fatigue, low mood, and poor recovery when depleted. After surgical recovery, ferritin depletion is particularly common.
HRT — a legitimate conversation at 38–39
Not a last resort. A precision tool that may be appropriate now.
Hormone replacement therapy has been reframed significantly by current evidence. For women under 60 or within 10 years of menopause onset, the benefit-to-risk ratio is strongly positive for most women without contraindications. Modern body-identical HRT (oestrogen patch or gel + micronised progesterone) carries a very different risk profile from the older synthetic progestogens that produced the negative headlines of the 1990s and 2000s.

At 38–39 with the symptom cluster Steph is describing — sleep disruption, mood dysregulation, cognitive fog, fatigue, joint changes, and the "not in my body" feeling — a knowledgeable GP or a menopause specialist (The Menopause Charity can signpost to good practitioners) will take these seriously and have a proper conversation. This is not about jumping to medication — it is about having access to the full picture of what is available.
What lifestyle does to hormones — the levers that are available now
Lifestyle is not a substitute for medical support but it is not irrelevant either.
Sleep → testosterone and progesterone: Both are synthesised overnight. 8 hours of sleep produces measurably higher levels of both than 6 hours. The sleep protocol is a direct hormonal intervention.

Resistance training → testosterone and GH: The strongest non-pharmaceutical testosterone stimulus in women is heavy resistance training, particularly of large muscle groups (glutes, quads, back). GH pulses during the post-training window and overnight.

Stress reduction → cortisol → testosterone: Cortisol and testosterone are inversely related. Chronically elevated cortisol suppresses testosterone production. Every stress management tool — Ashwagandha, Zone 2 cardio, sauna, cold exposure, magnesium — is a testosterone intervention.

Vitamin D3: D3 receptors are present in Leydig cells (where testosterone is produced — yes, women have these too) and in the hypothalamus. D3 deficiency directly impairs testosterone production. At UK latitude, from October to April, supplementation at 3,000 IU daily is the evidence-backed dose.
