Food synergies — combinations that work mechanistically
These are not food trends. Each combination produces a measurable biological effect beyond what either food achieves alone. Most are already built into your daily protocol — this explains why.
Collagen
+
Vitamin C
→
Doubles connective tissue collagen synthesis
Vitamin C is a required cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase — the enzyme that hydroxylates proline in collagen chains, a non-negotiable step in collagen cross-linking. Without it, collagen synthesis stalls. Shaw et al. (2019): this combination taken 45–60 minutes before exercise doubled collagen synthesis in tendons and ligaments compared to collagen alone or placebo. This is why collagen is only effective in the pre-training window — exercise increases the blood flow that delivers the amino acids to the tissue being loaded.
↑ 2× collagen synthesis · take 45–60 min before training
Turmeric
+
Black pepper
+
Fat
→
Increases curcumin absorption by up to 20× combined
Curcumin (the active compound in turmeric) has near-zero bioavailability on its own — it is poorly absorbed and rapidly metabolised. Piperine in black pepper inhibits glucuronidation in the intestinal wall, increasing absorption by roughly 20-fold. Dietary fat increases solubility (curcumin is fat-soluble). Use turmeric with eggs in olive oil, plus black pepper — you are now getting the actual anti-inflammatory dose, not a trace amount.
↑ ~20× curcumin absorption · applies to supplemental curcumin too
Vitamin D3
+
Dietary fat
→
Fat-soluble vitamins require fat — or they pass straight through
D3, K2, A, and E are fat-soluble — they are packaged into chylomicrons in the intestinal wall and require dietary fat to be incorporated. Taking D3 with a fat-free meal reduces absorption by ~50–70% in pharmacokinetic studies. This is why your D3+K2 timing always pairs with a fat-containing meal. The olive oil on eggs at breakfast is not incidental — it is the absorption vehicle.
↑ 2–3× D3 absorption · take with eggs, salmon, avocado
Iron-rich food
+
Vitamin C
→
Triples non-haem iron absorption
Non-haem iron (from plant foods and eggs) is absorbed at only 2–20% without enhancement. Vitamin C reduces ferric iron (Fe³⁺) to ferrous iron (Fe²⁺) — the absorbable form — and chelates it to prevent it binding to inhibitory phytates. Adding lemon juice or broccoli to a spinach-based dish can triple the iron absorbed from that meal. Iron is critical for oxygen transport and testosterone synthesis — relevant at 44 with heavy training load.
↑ 3× iron absorption · spinach + lemon · eggs + broccoli

Core timing principles
1
Carbs follow training demand, not clock time
Carbohydrates do not make you fat at night. They make you fat when consumed in excess of need. On training days, carbs at every meal are appropriate — you are loading glycogen for the next session. On rest days, reduce carbs by ~60g (primarily from the main meal) because glycogen demand is lower. This is not a calorie cut — protein and fat stay identical. Only the carb-to-fat ratio shifts.
2
Fat slows — use this as a tool, not a fear
Dietary fat slows gastric emptying. This is a negative before training (you want nutrients absorbed before lifting), a positive before sleep (casein + fat at night means sustained amino acid release for hours), and neutral at main meals when timing doesn't matter. The practical rule: keep fat low in the pre-training window, and use fat strategically in the evening meals and pre-sleep snack.
3
The 90-minute morning window is preparation, not delay
Training immediately on waking is training at biological low tide. Testosterone peaks mid-morning. Core body temperature rises over the first 90 minutes of waking. Neuromuscular activation improves across the same window. The 90 minutes between waking and training is the most valuable preparation available — more important than any supplement or warmup.
4
Thursday's food is Friday's session
Muscle glycogen replenishment takes 12–24 hours. Eating a large carbohydrate meal Friday morning does not meaningfully improve glycogen availability by the time you train at 07:30. Thursday's lunch and dinner are the primary fuel for Friday's posterior chain session. This is the most frequently missed timing insight in performance nutrition, and explains why elite athletes "carb load" the day before, not the morning of.
Satiety & energy — happy, full, and performing
A lean surplus means you are slightly undereating relative to maximum gain rate. The goal is to hit the targets without feeling depleted, hungry, or miserable. These are the tools that make the plan liveable — because the plan that is liveable is the plan that works.
Satiety — staying full
Satiety hack
Protein leverages every other meal
High-protein meals suppress ghrelin (the hunger hormone) more effectively than carbohydrate or fat-matched meals. 185g protein per day is not just a muscle-building target — it is a hunger management tool. If you hit protein at every meal, you will rarely feel truly hungry between them. The mechanism: protein stimulates PYY and GLP-1 (satiety hormones) and suppresses ghrelin for significantly longer than carbohydrates. When the plan feels hard to stick to, the first question to ask is: did you hit protein at every meal?
Hit protein first at every meal → hunger manages itself
Satiety hack
Volume eating — fill the plate, not the macros
Stomach stretch receptors register fullness before calorie receptors do. High-volume, low-calorie foods eaten alongside protein sources create physical fullness without using up calorie budget. The practical toolkit: unlimited spinach, rocket, cucumber, cherry tomatoes, mushrooms, courgette, celery, broccoli. These foods have almost no caloric consequence but occupy significant stomach volume. Eating 300g of salad alongside your chicken and rice means you finish the meal feeling genuinely full, not technically fed.
Unlimited: spinach · rocket · cucumber · courgette · mushrooms
Satiety hack
Greek yoghurt + berries + walnuts — the perfect snack architecture
This combination covers every satiety mechanism simultaneously: casein protein (slow-digesting, 5–7 hours), fat (slows gastric emptying), fibre from berries (gut motility), volume from yoghurt. It also delivers ~35g protein, antioxidants, omega-3 ALA, and polyphenols. At ~380 kcal it fits anywhere in the day. The critical detail: full-fat yoghurt is more satiating than low-fat — the fat is what makes the meal last.
250g full-fat yoghurt + 80g blueberries + 20g walnuts = 380 kcal · 35g protein · 5h satiety
Satiety hack
Slow eating is a legitimate performance strategy
Satiety hormones take 15–20 minutes to register after eating begins. People who eat quickly consume on average 15% more calories before feeling full. On a lean surplus where calorie precision matters, eating speed is a meaningful variable. The practical application: eat your protein first (it triggers satiety signals earliest), eat with a fork rather than standing at the counter, and pause between bites on the main meal. This is not precious — it is a mechanism.
Protein first · slow down by 30% → 15% fewer calories before satiety registers
Energy — performing all day
Energy hack
The 90-minute caffeine delay — your most underused tool
Cortisol peaks naturally in the first 60–90 minutes after waking. Drinking coffee immediately on waking means your caffeine peak coincides with the natural cortisol peak — producing tolerance faster and blunting the effect. Delaying your first coffee to 90 minutes post-wake means the caffeine peak coincides with the start of your training session, where you actually want it. This is the simplest single change for most morning trainers and produces a meaningfully better pre-training state from the same two flat whites.
Delay first coffee 90 min → natural cortisol peak + caffeine peak both hit at session start
Energy hack
Training day vs rest day energy feels different — on purpose
Rest day energy will be slightly lower than training days. This is not a problem — it is biology. Carbohydrate reduction on rest days means slightly less circulating glucose for energy, and lower training volume means less of the stimulant hormones (adrenaline, testosterone) being released. The correct response is not to try to recreate training-day energy on rest days through more caffeine or food. The correct response is to use the lower energy state for cognitive work, recovery protocols, and lower-intensity tasks. Rest day energy is not depleted — it is calibrated.
Rest day lower energy = correct physiology, not a problem to fix
Energy hack
The mid-afternoon dip is glycaemic — fix it with protein and fat, not carbs
The mid-afternoon energy dip (~14:00–15:00) has two causes: circadian rhythm (a genuine biological lull) and post-lunch blood glucose crash. Fixing the crash with more carbohydrates prolongs the blood sugar cycle. Fixing it with protein + fat produces a sustained energy state without the crash. The practical fix: replace the afternoon biscuit with Greek yoghurt + walnuts. The protein and fat slow glucose release, the casein sustains it, and the walnuts provide alpha-linolenic acid for brain function.
Afternoon dip → protein + fat snack, not sugar
Sustainability — making it last 12 weeks
Sustainability
The one-rule Sunday — protein only, everything else yours
Sunday is deliberately the plan's most flexible day. The psychological research on dietary adherence is consistent: complete restriction produces a "what-the-hell effect" — one transgression leads to complete abandonment. One day of dietary flexibility per week, within a clear single constraint (hit your protein), dramatically improves full-week adherence. The Sunday rule is not a cheat day — it is a designed pressure valve. Eat what you enjoy. The only rule: 180g protein, distributed through the day.
One rule Sunday = better 6-day adherence, not worse
Sustainability
When you're over chicken — the protein swap protocol
Week 3 of any programme, the monotony of a fixed protein source becomes the biggest adherence risk. The food shuffle button in the Today page exists for exactly this moment. The key principle: any protein source that clears the leucine threshold (~3g leucine per portion) is nutritionally interchangeable for MPS purposes. Swap chicken for: salmon (same protein, adds omega-3), turkey (similar profile, different flavour), whole eggs (complete, fast-digesting), tuna (leanest option), sirloin or lean beef (adds zinc, iron, and creatine precursors — 2–3× per week is optimal for testosterone support), white fish like cod or sea bass (highest protein-to-calorie ratio of any whole food — the rest day protein of choice), haddock (same profile, more flavour). The science does not care about the flavour. Your brain does. Rotate freely.
Chicken → salmon → sirloin → cod → turkey → eggs → sea bass — all hit the leucine threshold · each adds different micronutrients
Sustainability
Eating at restaurants — the 80% rule
Restaurant meals will happen. The goal is not to avoid them — it is to make them a ~15–20% variance rather than a session-cancelling event. The 80% rule: ensure 80% of the meal is protein and vegetables, and be less precise about the rest. Practical framework: at any restaurant, order protein + vegetables as the primary. Bread, sauce, and alcohol are the variables. A steak + greens + olive oil + glass of wine is effectively on-plan. A pizza + three beers is a different conversation. The choice is available at almost any restaurant — it just requires choosing it.
Protein + vegetables first, variables second → restaurants are not a problem

Context-dependent — read the rule
🛢️
Refined seed oils — sunflower, vegetable, corn, rapeseed
High linoleic acid (omega-6) competes directly with EPA/DHA for the same COX enzymes your supplement stack is working to down-regulate. You cannot suppress the inflammatory pathway with omega-3s while simultaneously flooding it with omega-6. Seed oils are in almost every restaurant kitchen, most ready meals, and most processed sauces. At home: olive oil and butter only. Eating out: this is unavoidable and acceptable — it's the daily home cooking that matters.
Always
🍺
Alcohol on training days — any amount
Ethanol inhibits mTOR directly, independent of calories or sleep. 1–2 drinks post-training: ~15% MPS suppression. 3–4 drinks: ~25%. 5+: near-total cancellation of the session's anabolic signal. The mechanism is direct phosphorylation interference with the mTOR pathway — not indirect via sleep disruption (though that also applies). If drinking, do it on full rest days, never within 6 hours of training. The post-workout protein window non-negotiable: protein before the first drink, every time.
Training days
🦠
Ultra-processed protein sources — most protein bars, deli meats, processed sausages
Most protein bars use hydrolysed collagen, soy isolate, or whey blend profiles that do not provide sufficient leucine to clear the mTOR activation threshold. A bar claiming 20g protein may only deliver 1.2g leucine — below the 2–3g required to trigger MPS. Additionally, nitrites in deli meats convert to nitrosamines and are associated with systemic inflammation. Whole food protein sources (chicken, eggs, fish, Greek yoghurt) have superior amino acid profiles and no additives. Convenience is not worth MPS suppression.
Always
🍭
High-fructose foods on rest days — fruit juice, honey, agave, corn syrup
Fructose is metabolised exclusively in the liver — it cannot be stored as muscle glycogen. On rest days when muscle glycogen demand is low, excess fructose converts to liver fat (de novo lipogenesis) and drives triglyceride synthesis. On training days directly before a session, the picture changes: a small amount of fructose (bananas, berries) is fine because muscle glycogen demand is high and liver throughput is not the bottleneck. Rule: whole fruit fine always, fruit juice and syrups contextually.
Rest days
🍖
Large meals within 90 min of training
Fat and fibre significantly slow gastric emptying. A high-fat, high-fibre meal within 90 minutes of compound lifting means blood is diverted to digestion during the session, RPE increases, and nausea risk rises under heavy loading. The pre-workout meal is specifically engineered to be fast-digesting (whey + banana + dates, or eggs without fat) precisely because gastric emptying speed affects performance. The main meal after training is when fat is entirely appropriate. Timing the fat accordingly is the difference between a 95% session and a 100% session.
Pre-training
☕
Coffee immediately on waking — the cortisol mistake
Cortisol peaks naturally in the first 60–90 minutes after waking as part of the cortisol awakening response (CAR). Drinking coffee during this window means caffeine peaks when cortisol is already high — producing tolerance faster, increasing afternoon cortisol resistance, and contributing to the mid-afternoon energy crash. The fix: water first, then 90 minutes of food + hydration + movement, then coffee. Two flat whites before training remains entirely fine — the timing shift alone changes the quality of the effect significantly. Your two oat milk flat whites: move them 60–90 minutes later for a meaningfully better outcome.
First 90 min
🛏️
Eating within 60–90 minutes of sleep
Late eating raises core body temperature and insulin, both of which interfere with the drop in core temperature that triggers slow-wave sleep onset. Late insulin spikes also suppress GH release — the primary hormonal signal for overnight muscle repair and connective tissue remodelling. The exception and intentional exception: cottage cheese before bed is high casein/low caloric impact and does not significantly raise insulin. The rule applies to large carbohydrate or calorie-dense meals, not the pre-sleep casein snack.
