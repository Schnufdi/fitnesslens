06:00 — Morning ignition sequence
06:00
Wake. No phone. 500ml water immediately.
The first thing the nervous system receives sets the neurochemical tone for the next 4 hours. Phone = dopamine fragmentation before the cortisol awakening response has completed. Water = gut motility activation, dehydration reversal, ghrelin management. Non-negotiable order: water before anything else.
06:05
10 minutes outside. Eyes toward sky. No sunglasses.
Retinal photoreceptors signal the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Cortisol awakening response is anchored. Serotonin synthesis begins in the raphe nuclei. Circadian timer starts. This single act improves sleep onset by 1–2 hours tonight, sharpens the morning cognitive window, and raises serotonin baseline for the day. Takes 10 minutes. Costs nothing.
06:15
Hip flexor stretch 90s/side + thoracic rotation + ankle circles. Every morning.
Tight hip flexors inhibit glute activation through reciprocal inhibition — the single most common reason hip thrusts feel weak despite adequate load. This 8-minute sequence, done every morning without exception, produces measurable improvements in glute recruitment within 10 days and directly protects the meniscal environment by restoring correct pelvic alignment.
06:30
Cold finish on the shower. 30–60 seconds. Every training morning.
Noradrenaline surge of 200–300%. Dopamine elevation lasting 2–4 hours. Cortisol baseline reduction over weeks of consistent practice. The HPA axis learns that acute stress is manageable — this is anxiety inoculation through deliberate discomfort. The effect compounds. After 3 weeks it becomes the morning's most anticipated moment rather than its most dreaded one.
07:30
First coffee. With 200mg L-Theanine. Not before.
Cortisol has peaked and is declining. Caffeine now amplifies the alerting signal rather than duplicating it — the effect is measurably stronger, lasts longer, and does not produce the 11am crash that early coffee creates. L-Theanine produces alpha brain wave state alongside the caffeine stimulation: alert without wired, focused without anxious. This combination is the cleanest cognitive performance stack available before training.

In the gym — what the machine demands
The non-negotiable rule
Progressive overload, every session, every week
If the weight, reps, or density did not increase this week versus last week, the session did not count for muscle growth. The body adapts to the stimulus it received — not the one you planned. Track every working set. Log every number. The log is the programme. Without progressive overload there is stimulus maintenance, not growth. This is the line between training and exercising.
Log every set. Increase something every session. When you cannot — deload, then push harder.
The execution standard
Full range, controlled eccentric, 2 second pause on key lifts
Half reps produce half the stimulus. The eccentric (lowering) phase produces more mechanical tension than the concentric — slowing it to 2–3 seconds on hip thrusts, RDLs, and rows doubles the effective stimulus per set without adding a single extra set. A 2-second pause at the top of the hip thrust forces a held contraction under load — the highest-quality glute stimulus available per rep. Standard matters more than load. Ego loads with poor range are the primary cause of plateau.
2–3s eccentric. Pause at peak contraction. Every rep of every working set.
The Friday directive
Posterior chain Friday is the most important session of the week
Hip thrusts, RDLs, step-ups, and hip thrust finisher generate the largest acute testosterone and GH response of any session in the programme. They directly address the glute weakness that is both the aesthetic goal and the therapeutic priority for knee biomechanics. Everything in the week — Thursday's carb-load, Thursday's sleep, Thursday's no-alcohol rule — exists to make Friday optimal. Protect Friday above all other sessions.
Thursday is Friday's preparation. Arrive fuelled, rested, and ready to push the hip thrust number.
The knee protocol
The modification is the programme — not a limitation of it
RDL replaces squat. Partial leg press (0–70°) replaces full depth. Low box step-up replaces lunge. These are not compromises — they deliver 97% of the original stimulus while the meniscal environment recovers and strengthens. Collagen timing, swimming, hip flexor stretching, and Zone 2 cardio are all active therapeutic interventions. You are not training around an injury. You are training the injury back to function.
The modified programme is the optimal programme for your current biology. Run it fully.

The order is the intervention. It requires no willpower — just sequencing.

Sauna · dry heat
Post-training. 15–20 min. 80–90°C.
The sauna is one of the most potent non-pharmaceutical longevity and recovery tools available. The evidence base is substantial — Finnish cohort studies show sauna use 4–7× weekly reduces cardiovascular mortality by 50% and all-cause mortality by 40%. The mechanisms relevant to your programme:

GH surge. A single 15-minute sauna session at 80°C produces a GH pulse of up to 16× baseline — one of the largest acute GH stimuli outside of sleep. Timing it post-training (when the training-induced GH pulse has cleared) produces an additive second pulse rather than competing with the first.

Heat shock proteins. Heat stress activates HSP70 and HSP90 — chaperone proteins that repair damaged proteins in muscle cells and protect against cellular damage from training. This is the molecular basis of the accelerated recovery sauna users consistently report.

Cardiovascular adaptation. The heart rate response to sauna (typically 100–150 bpm) produces cardiovascular conditioning that partially substitutes for Zone 2 cardio — particularly useful on days when additional cardio volume would exceed recovery capacity.

Mental health. Dynorphin release during heat stress initially produces discomfort, then triggers a subsequent upregulation of mu-opioid receptors — the mechanism behind the profound relaxation and euphoria that follows a sauna session. This is the same receptor system as the runner's high. The effect lasts 2–4 hours post-session.
Protocol: Enter 10–15 min post-training. 15–20 min at 80–90°C. Exit. 2–3 min cool-down (shower or fresh air). Optional second round of 10 min. Hydrate with 500ml electrolyte water before and after. Never sauna when significantly dehydrated.
Knee note: Heat increases synovial fluid viscosity and promotes collagen synthesis in connective tissue. For the meniscal environment, post-training sauna use is therapeutic — not contraindicated. The heat accelerates the same collagen repair that the pre-training collagen + Vit C protocol initiates.
Steam room · wet heat
Pre-training or rest days. 10–15 min. 40–45°C.
The steam room operates at lower temperatures than the sauna (40–45°C vs 80–90°C) but at 100% humidity, producing a different physiological response more suited to specific timing windows.

Pre-training use. 10 minutes of steam before training elevates muscle temperature 1–2°C above resting — which increases muscle fibre elasticity, reduces the viscosity of synovial fluid, and improves joint range of motion. This is the most effective warm-up available for mobility and injury prevention, particularly relevant to the knee. The collagen in the meniscus is more pliable at elevated temperature — reducing the mechanical stress per rep in the early sets.

Respiratory function. Steam inhalation reduces airway resistance and clears the upper respiratory tract — producing a measurable improvement in breathing efficiency during subsequent training. For swimming, pre-swim steam is particularly effective at preparing the respiratory system.

Cortisol management. The wet heat environment activates the parasympathetic nervous system more rapidly than dry sauna — making it more effective as an acute stress and anxiety intervention when the priority is nervous system calming rather than the GH and heat shock protein stimulus of the dry sauna.

Skin and connective tissue. The humidity keeps collagen hydrated and pliable. On rest days, a 15-minute steam session promotes connective tissue recovery through improved circulation without the cardiovascular load of dry sauna.
Protocol A (pre-training): 8–10 min steam → cold shower 60s → train. The contrast between wet heat and cold sharpens sympathetic activation for the session — the hormonal equivalent of pressing a reset button before lifting.
Protocol B (rest day recovery): 15 min steam → 5 min cool-down → swim or hydro pool. This sequence — steam, cool, water immersion — is the most complete soft tissue and nervous system recovery protocol available in your gym in a single visit.
Hydro pool · hydrotherapy
Post-training or rest days. 15–20 min.
The hydro pool is the most directly therapeutic tool in your gym for the meniscal environment — and the most underused by almost everyone. Warm water immersion with jet hydrotherapy produces effects that no other modality can replicate.

Hydrostatic pressure and joint unloading. Immersion at chest depth reduces effective body weight by approximately 70%. The knee joint — which bears roughly 3× bodyweight during walking and up to 8× during heavy training — is unloaded to near-zero. In this environment, gentle active movement of the knee through its full available range (0–70° initially, progressing as the protocol advances) feeds the joint without loading it. This is the most effective cartilage nutrition protocol available — articular cartilage has no blood supply and receives nutrients only through compression and decompression during movement.

Jet hydrotherapy. The water jets provide localised massage — increasing blood flow, reducing inflammation markers, accelerating metabolite clearance from muscle tissue, and improving lymphatic drainage. The combination of hydrostatic unloading and jet stimulation is more effective for acute recovery than either ice bath or massage alone according to multiple sports science studies.

Thermal effect. Warm water (typically 34–38°C in hydro pools) maintains elevated muscle temperature longer than post-training air exposure, keeping the tissue pliable during the recovery window when collagen synthesis is most active. Combined with the pre-training collagen + Vit C protocol, post-training hydro pool use extends the collagen synthesis window further.
Post-training protocol: Enter hydro pool within 20 min of finishing the session. 15–20 min. Focus jets on quads, hamstrings, glutes. Gentle knee movement (small circles, 0–60° flexion) in the buoyancy-assisted environment. Do not rush — this window is the active recovery session, not a passive soak.
Rest day protocol: Steam 10 min → hydro pool 20 min → swim 20–30 min. This sequence on a Tuesday or Thursday is the most complete recovery session your gym can provide — connective tissue, nervous system, cardiovascular, and joint nutrition all addressed in under 90 minutes.
The contrast protocol — the most powerful combination your gym enables
Sauna → cold shower → hydro pool. Post-training on training days, or as a standalone on rest days.
Contrast therapy — alternating heat and cold — produces a vascular pumping effect through the peripheral vasculature. Heat causes vasodilation (blood vessels expand, blood floods the periphery). Cold causes vasoconstriction (vessels contract, blood is driven back toward the core). Repeated alternation creates a forced circulatory cycle that accelerates metabolite clearance, reduces delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), and delivers fresh oxygenated blood to the recovering tissue.

The specific sequence for your gym: 15 min sauna → 60–90s cold shower → 15 min hydro pool. Repeat once if time permits. The sauna produces the GH pulse and heat shock proteins. The cold shower resets the sympathetic system and drives blood centrally. The hydro pool provides the joint-specific unloading and jet-assisted recovery that completes the cycle.

This sequence takes approximately 45–50 minutes post-training and produces a recovery effect that would otherwise require 24–48 hours of passive rest. Elite athletes pay for access to facilities that do exactly this. You have it in your gym.
Sauna → cold → hydro pool. The full contrast protocol. Use it at least once a week — ideally post-Friday's posterior chain session.

Why you need all of them — the gaps they fill
Each modality addresses a deficit the others create. This is why elite performers use multiple disciplines rather than one — not for variety, but because the body genuinely needs inputs that no single modality provides.
1
Gym builds the muscle — swimming protects the joint that carries it
Heavy resistance training creates mechanical stress on joints as an unavoidable consequence of producing the overload stimulus that drives muscle growth. Swimming on rest days creates the opposite condition: full-body movement, zero joint compression, synovial circulation without loading. The combination produces something neither achieves alone: muscular development in a joint environment that is actively recovering rather than accumulating stress. This is particularly relevant to the meniscal tear — swimming is therapeutic, not just supplementary.
Gym drives adaptation · Swimming enables it to continue
2
Step machine builds the aerobic base — running activates it for tennis
The step machine builds Zone 2 mitochondrial density and glute conditioning safely within Phase 1 constraints. Running takes that cardiovascular base and converts it into sport-specific fitness — the lateral loading, the impact patterns, the stride mechanics that tennis requires. You cannot run immediately because the knee needs the preparatory phase. But every step machine session is building the aerobic foundation that will make your eventual running (and tennis return) more capable than if you had been sedentary. The step machine is not a substitute for running — it is the prerequisite for it.
Step machine now · Running when the gate opens
3
Mobility isn't recovery — it's the hidden performance multiplier
Tight hip flexors reduce glute activation through reciprocal inhibition. Limited thoracic rotation reduces the power transfer in a tennis groundstroke. Restricted ankle mobility changes knee mechanics under load. Ten minutes of mobility work daily — hip flexor stretching, thoracic rotation, ankle circles — produces measurable improvements in the quality of every gym session, every step machine session, and eventually every tennis session. It is the intervention with the highest carry-over to everything else, and the one most consistently skipped because it does not feel like training.
The multiplier that makes every other modality work better
4
Swimming is the mental reset no other modality provides
Swimming in water produces a measurable parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) nervous system response — the combination of sensory restriction, breath-regulated rhythm, and hydrostatic pressure reduces cortisol and activates the vagus nerve more reliably than any land-based exercise. At 44, with significant professional and life demands, a 30-minute swim once or twice a week is not just physical training — it is one of the most powerful cortisol management tools available. The anxiety, stress, and nervous system chapters all point toward this same result through different mechanisms. Swimming achieves it through direct physiological action.
The cortisol reset · anxiety intervention · recovery tool

How the pieces interact — the logic behind the schedule
1
Thursday is the most strategically important non-training day of the week
Thursday determines Friday. Sleep quality, glycogen loading (the carb-heavy Thursday lunch), swimming for joint decompression, and the hip flexor mobility work all serve one purpose: arriving at Friday's posterior chain session in the best possible physiological state. The step machine on Tuesday depletes glycogen slightly, making Thursday's carb load more effective — a mild glycogen supercompensation effect going into the week's most important session. Thursday evening: no alcohol, early bed, magnesium glycinate. The system is designed around Friday.
Thursday is Friday's preparation
2
Swimming goes on rest days — specifically because they're rest days
The physiological benefit of swimming for recovery depends on it occurring when the muscular and nervous system stress from resistance training is at its highest — the day after a session. Tuesday swimming follows Monday's push session. Thursday swimming follows Wednesday's pull session. The joint decompression, synovial circulation, and parasympathetic activation are most valuable precisely when the gym has created the conditions that need them. Swimming on a training day would reduce its recovery value significantly and create additional fatigue before the next session.
Rest day swimming amplifies recovery from training day gym
3
Sunday is the reset — not just a rest day
The one-rule Sunday nutrition protocol (protein only, everything else flexible) serves psychological recovery as much as physiological. The yin yoga session on Sunday produces the mobility improvements that persist through the following training week. Sunday swimming, when it happens, should be unhurried — recreational rather than structured. The goal is parasympathetic reset, not cardiovascular adaptation. The body needs one day per week where effort is absent and recovery is the only agenda.
Sunday is active recovery, not additional training
4
The nutrition system is already integrated with the activity system
Training day calories (2,950 kcal) vs rest day calories (2,700 kcal) vs Thursday carb-load vs Friday peak (3,050 kcal) are not arbitrary — they are calculated against the energy demands of each day's specific activity combination. When swimming is added on Tuesday or Thursday, the caloric requirement adjusts: a 30-minute swim burns approximately 250–350 kcal depending on intensity and stroke. On days combining step machine and swimming, total expenditure increases meaningfully — eat to target, not to restriction. The surplus goal (+200 kcal) applies to net intake after all activity expenditure.
Add swimming → add ~250–350 kcal to that day's target

Anxiety & stress — the biology, the levers, the protocol

1
The HPA axis is the anxiety machine — and it is directly modifiable
The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the primary stress response system. In response to perceived threat (real or psychological), the hypothalamus releases CRH, which triggers ACTH from the pituitary, which triggers cortisol from the adrenal glands. Cortisol then activates the sympathetic nervous system, producing the physical symptoms of anxiety: racing heart, tight chest, shallow breathing, heightened vigilance. Chronic anxiety is chronic HPA axis hyperactivation — a system stuck in elevated alert. This is not a character failing. The five metrics that tell you the machine is running well
1
You wake naturally before or at your alarm
Sleep architecture is completing full cycles. GH has pulsed. Cortisol awakening response is initiating naturally. The body is not being dragged out of a cycle by an alarm — it is completing the cycle. This is the most reliable single indicator that sleep is working correctly.
2
Hunger arrives gently 3–4h after meals
Ghrelin rhythm is trained. Leptin is stable. Protein targets are being hit. If you are ravenous between meals or not hungry at meal times, the ghrelin system is not in its optimal state — investigate sleep, protein targets, or meal timing first.
3
Type 4 Bristol Stool most mornings
Transit time is optimal. Fibre, hydration, and gut motility are working. Microbiome is in reasonable health. The most immediate daily diagnostic available — and the one most people never think to use.
4
Resting morning heart rate stable or improving over weeks
Cardiovascular fitness is improving. Nervous system recovery is adequate. HRV (if tracked) is the more sensitive version of this metric. Elevated resting HR is an early signal of inadequate recovery, overtraining, or illness — before any subjective symptoms appear.
5
Hip thrust weight is moving upward weekly
The single most reliable composite indicator of whether the entire system is working — sleep, nutrition, hormones, recovery, and training all need to be functioning correctly for the hip thrust to progress. When it stalls, something in the system has failed. When it is moving, everything is working.
